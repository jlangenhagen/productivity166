Puzzle 3 / 4 · Definition of Done

What counts as "Done"?

Task: "Literature research for next paper". Tick all conditions that must be fulfilled for this task to be truly Done.

The workshop asked: "When is a task actually done?" A clear Definition of Done removes ambiguity and prevents tasks from staying stuck in In Progress. Here is why each condition is correct or not for "Literature research for next paper":

✓ Number of articles to review has been defined
Without a target number, you never know when to stop searching. The workshop explicitly listed this as one of the open questions for this task.

✗ The paper has already been written
Writing the paper is an entirely separate task. Including it here would mix two distinct workflow steps — the Definition of Done must match the scope of this task only.

✓ Keywords for database search have been decided
Without defined keywords the search is neither systematic nor reproducible. The workshop listed "Which keywords?" as a key open question for this task.

✗ A target conference has been identified
Choosing a conference belongs to the planning of the paper's publication — a separate task. Literature research is complete before that decision is made.

✓ Databases to be searched have been selected
"Which databases?" was explicitly listed in the workshop. Searching without a defined set of databases produces inconsistent results.

✗ Revision and proofreading is finished
Revision belongs to a writing or editing task — it presupposes a draft that doesn't exist yet at the research stage.

✓ Decision made on whether reading is included in scope
"Literature research" is ambiguous: does it mean finding sources, or also reading them? The workshop explicitly raised this question — the scope must be clarified upfront.

✓ Scope confirmed with supervisor
In a PhD context, the supervisor defines expectations. A task cannot be "Done" if the supervisor hasn't agreed on what "Done" means for this research task.

Puzzle 4 / 4 · Habit Stacking

Which formulation is correct?

The workshop introduced Habit Stacking with the template: "After [current habit] I will [new habit]." Which of the following is correctly formulated?

A
"I want to read more at some point." — no anchor, no time
B
"After my morning coffee, I will read my textbook for 10 minutes." — correct format with clear anchor
C
"I will stop checking emails first thing in the morning." — negatively framed, no new behavior
D
"Sometimes after sport I might read something." — non-committal, no fixed anchor
From the workshop: "Habit Stacking: After [current habit] I will [new habit]." The template requires a concrete existing habit as the anchor (the cue) and a specific new behavior.
• Option A has no anchor and no concrete timing.
• Option C defines what NOT to do instead of what TO do — it's not a habit stacking formula.
• Option D uses "sometimes" and "might" — too vague to trigger a reliable cue.
Option B is the correct format: clear existing habit (morning coffee) + specific new behavior (10-min reading).
